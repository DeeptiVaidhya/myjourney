What will happen if I have genomic tumor testing?
Here’s what you can expect if you decide to undergo genomic tumor testing.

For genomic tumor testing, your oncologist will need a sample of your blood and likely a sample of your tumor. Many times, your oncologist can use a sample of your tumor from a previous biopsy. If not, you will need to have another biopsy. Once your tumor sample and blood sample are ready, they will be sent to a laboratory for testing. This testing usually takes 2-4 weeks. Once the results are ready, they will be sent to your oncologist. Your oncologist will review the results and set up a time to discuss them with you, either by phone or in person.

Things to consider about genomic tumor testing
Like most medical procedures, there are many things to consider.

As you read through the following sections, make note of the topics that you would like to talk about with your oncologist during your next visit.

Could my cancer treatment plan change as a result of genomic tumor testing?

It is a real possibility that your treatment plan may change after genomic tumor testing. Indeed, the goal of genomic tumor testing is to find tumor mutations and identify treatment options. However, there is no guarantee that this will happen. We won’t know if new or different treatment options are available for you until the results are back.

What are the risks of genomic tumor testing?

To do genomic tumor testing, your oncologist will need a sample of your tumor. Many times, your oncologist can use a sample of your tumor from a previous biopsy. However, if your oncologist needs another sample of your tumor, you will need to have another biopsy. There may be risks to having a biopsy, especially if it requires surgery. Your oncologist and surgeon can explain any risks related to having a biopsy.

Another potential risk to consider is that the results of your genomic tumor testing will become a part of your medical record. Information in your medical record can be viewed by a third party such as an insurance company (such as life insurance or health insurance) if you allow them to access to your medical record.

Will my insurance cover the cost of genomic tumor testing?

Because genomic tumor testing is still new and changing as more research is done, it is not always considered the standard of care. Your insurance company many not cover some or all of the costs associated with genomic tumor testing. Your oncologist or a representative from the cancer center will talk to you about the potential costs as well as payment options if your insurance does not cover the costs.

Are there limits to precision oncology?

Precision oncology is a new science and there is still a lot to learn about how genomic tumor testing can improve cancer treatment. Not all patients who undergo genomic tumor testing will have a mutation, and not all mutations can be matched with known treatment options. Therefore, not all patients will benefit from genomic tumor testing. In fact, only a small portion of cancer patients will likely benefit from genomic tumor testing. Only you and your oncologist can decide if you are a good candidate.

What is a mutation and how did I get one?
“Mutation” might sound like a scary word, but it gives us more information about what makes you unique.

Each of us has a unique set of DNA, or chemical blueprints, that determine how our bodies look and function. DNA is found inside every cell in the body. The segments of DNA that contain instructions for making specific body proteins are called genes. A mutation is an error in the DNA that makes up a gene.

Mutations fall into two major categories: hereditary mutations and acquired mutations.

What is a hereditary mutation?

Hereditary mutations are inherited, or passed down through families, similar to eye color and hair color. They are also called germline mutations because they are found in the parent’s egg or sperm cells (germ cells). Hereditary mutations are present throughout a person’s life and they can be found in every cell in the body.

Hereditary mutations can be identified through genetic testing. This sounds similar to genomic testing, but is a different kind of test. For example, the test for BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes is a genetic test of inherited mutations that can help predict your risk for getting breast or ovarian cancer.

Not all hereditary mutations are passed on to the next generation. Also, hereditary mutations are not always harmful; they can also be beneficial or have no effect at all.

The purpose of genomic tumor testing is NOT to look for hereditary mutations. Read the next section (acquired mutations) to learn about the type of mutations that genomic tumor testing looks for.

What if my genomic tumor testing finds a hereditary mutation that was passed down to me?

Although the purpose of genomic tumor testing is NOT to look for hereditary mutations, some types of genomic tumor testing may also detect them. This can depend on the type of test ordered and the laboratory where the samples are processed.

If your genomic tumor testing finds a hereditary mutation, you will be referred to a genetic counselor to help you or your family understand what this means for you.

What is an acquired mutation?

Acquired (or somatic) mutations are not inherited or passed down through families. They occur at some time during a person’s life and they are only present in certain cells, not in every cell in the body.

Acquired mutations happen randomly. For example, acquired mutations may occur if there is an error, like a typo, while DNA copies itself during cell division.

Each person’s cancer has a unique combination of acquired mutations. Genomic tumor testing can help doctors identify acquired mutations in a patient’s cancer. Then, they can try to match those mutations to treatments that target those mutations.
